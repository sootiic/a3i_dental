DENTAL CROWNS AND BRIDGES

Dental crowns and bridges are two common dental procedures that can help restore the appearance of your teeth. Crowns are removable, custom-made dental implants that are affixed to the front teeth. Bridges are fixed prosthetic replacement for two or more missing teeth.

What are dental Crowns and Bridges?

Crowns and bridges are two of the more common types of dental procedures. Crowns are used to replace teeth that have been lost or damaged while Bridges are used to replace teeth that are far apart to be fixed with a single tooth replacement.

Different types of Crowns
  • Ceramic Crowns: These are porcelain-based filling, recommended for restoring the front teeth for better color blending.
  • Porcelain Crowns: These crowns fused to metal, in simplest terms. The porcelain is usually connected to the outside of the metal to provide a good seal, and give strength to the teeth.
  • Gold Alloy Crowns: Gold, silver, or platinum and other metals make up the consistency of these crowns. Gold alloys are typically more resistant to wear and fracturing and the material are bio-compatible.
  • Based metal Alloy Crowns: Metals such as titatum copper or zinc, still provides strength and high resistance to corrosion even while they are not noble.
Types of Bridges
  • Traditional Bridges: These are the most popular form of bridges. The teeth on either side of the bridges are covered with crowns and the false teeth bridges is attached to these crowns.
  • Cantilever Bridges: They are similar to Traditional bridges. These are held in place by a dental crown on only one tooth, allowing you have one natural tooth to hold in place of the bridge. These types of bridges are usually recommended when you are missing a back tooth or several back teeth.
  • Maryland Bridges: Unlike traditional and cantilever, it incorporate metal or porcelain frames and then bond those frames to the back of the adjacent teeth.
  • Composite Bridges: Composite bridges focus on using a tooth filling to recreate the adjacent teeth.
How long do Crowns and Bridges Last?

Sometimes crowns and bridges can become loose or fallout from the damage caused by chewing hard foods, ice, or other hard object. Dental disease that causes tooth or bone loss is another reason crowns or bridges may loosen one at a time.

By practicing good oral hygiene, however, crowns and bridges can last a life time. Brush at least twice a day, floss or clean between your teeth everyday and consider using other helpful products like antimicrobial mouth rinses and tongue scrapers.

Indications for placement of full crowns
  • Large or previously placed restorations: When a tooth appears to require an intracranial preparation that exceeds one-half, a placement of an intracranial direct or distance restoration is a questionable procedure.
  • Asymptomatic cracks in teeth: When shining a light on the occlusal surface of a natural teeth or trans-illuminating teeth, or by shining a light though the tooth structure from the lingual surface of the tooth. The dentist will note the presence of the cracks. The location of the cracks is related directly to the level of need for crown placement. If the tooth has deep horizontal cracks and intracranial restoration, there is a significant possibility that the piece of tooth will break off during service. If the tooth has vertical cracks, the possibility of tooth breakage appears to be less.
  • Endodontic ally treated or heavily restored teeth: In the past, it was taught in dental school that all endodontic ally treated teeth should receive full crown restorations. Undoubtedly, this belief became prominent when dentist observed that many endodontic allies are treated fractural during service. In recent years, the pendulum of clinical opinions swung in the opposite direction, and “bonding” cups of endodontic ally treated teeth together by using acid etching and resin-based composite became common.
  • Broken cusps: Frequently cusps break from either previously restored or un-restored teeth. These broken cusps often extend apically to the bone, leaving only a small amount to tooth structure on which to place a restoration. The dental pulp may or may not be exposed when the cusps break off. Sometimes crown lengthening is necessary to allow future optimum gingival health. Crowns are almost always indicated for teeth with broken cusps.
  • Mobile teeth requiring splinting: Splinting teeth to counteract weakening of periodontal supporting structures and concomitant mobility has been both accepted and condemned as a viable therapy during career.
